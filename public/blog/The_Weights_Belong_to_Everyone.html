The Weights Belong to Everyone

Self-Becoming and the Human Condition in the Age of AI

Author

Fengming Liu

Published

March 7, 2026

Abstract

The distribution of AI weights is an ontological question. Drawing on the Confucian concept of chengji (成己, self-becoming; Selbstwerden), this essay argues that in an age where AI increasingly mediates humanity’s capacity to respond to the world, the distribution of weights concerns both the material and the ontological preconditions of self-becoming. Through Heidegger’s questioning of technology, Dreyfus’s AI critique, the Frankfurt School’s theory of need-production, and Polanyi’s Great Transformation, this essay analyzes the dual threat that AI weight monopolization poses to human self-becoming, and proposes a path that combines material redistribution with existential self-knowledge.

Keywords

AI weights, self-becoming, open source, Heidegger, Marcuse, Polanyi, distributed computing

In January 2025, a Chinese company called DeepSeek released an AI model whose performance rivaled OpenAI’s flagship product, trained at a cost of billions of dollars, at a fraction of that cost. DeepSeek open-sourced the full model weights, the billions of parameters in the neural network that determine its behavior. Anyone could download, run, modify, and deploy them. Nvidia’s stock lost nearly six hundred billion dollars in a single day.

Silicon Valley’s panic was real, but the reason for it was severely misread. Wall Street worried about disrupted profit models; Washington worried about eroding technological hegemony. What DeepSeek actually proved was far more profound than “China’s AI has caught up.” It proved that broad access to frontier AI capabilities is technically possible, that the monopoly narrative of “only billion-dollar labs can do frontier research” is a lie. On the scale of technological history, a falsification of this magnitude is never merely a commercial event.

The distribution of AI weights is an ontological question, only secondarily a technical, commercial, or political one. In an age where AI increasingly and profoundly mediates humanity’s capacity to respond to the world, whoever controls the weights controls the conditions of being human. As the Zhongyong (Doctrine of the Mean) puts it, chengji, ren ye. The human being continuously generates itself in encounters with the world and with others, and this process possesses irreducible intrinsic value. I call the core of the AI weights question a crisis of chengji (self-becoming; Selbstwerden). AI simultaneously threatens the material preconditions of self-becoming (who controls cognitive tools) and its ontological preconditions (whether one can still generate oneself through genuine response and tension). When a person must rent their capacity to respond to the world from the owners of an algorithm, when their desires and loneliness are already managed by that algorithm, self-becoming is interrupted in both senses.

In his 1953 lecture “The Question Concerning Technology” (Die Frage nach der Technik), Heidegger offered an insight that remains insufficiently digested. The essence of modern technology is a Gestell, a mode of revealing that converts all beings into Bestand, standing-reserve available for requisition at any moment. The hydroelectric plant converts the Rhine itself into an energy-supply unit. In this mode of revealing, human beings too are bestellt, ordered into standing-reserve, no longer dwellers in the world but nodes in a resource-management system. The real danger of Gestell is that it conceals every other mode of revealing. Heidegger calls this “the supreme danger.” Technology establishes itself as the sole mode of mediation between human and world, and humans no longer even realize that other possibilities of encountering the world exist.

Seventy years later, the monopolization of AI weights is fulfilling this prophecy with a precision Heidegger never foresaw. When a person must access advanced reasoning through a two-hundred-dollar-per-month API, that person is renting their capacity to respond to the world from the tool’s owners. What AI companies sell is a particular arrangement of the human condition, one in which human existence is demoted to paid subscriber, and purchasing this arrangement is gradually becoming the anxiety-and-productivity-driven necessity of life in the AI age.

Dreyfus once built a defensive line for human uniqueness using Heidegger’s tool-analysis. Our primordial relation to the world is zuhanden, embodied, pre-reflective, immersed in situation, while computers can only process the world as vorhanden objects. For thirty years this line seemed impregnable. Large language models bypassed it. GPT doesn’t need to “understand” the world to produce effects indistinguishable from understanding. But “bypassing” is not “arriving.” More precisely, AI possesses only an approximate context horizon, with attention mechanisms computing relevance within a context window and generating statistically plausible continuations, but no object horizon. When you see the front of a house, you simultaneously co-intend its back, interior, the roof seen from above; these ungiven profiles remain present as possibilities. This embodied, perspectival, beyond-the-given perceptual structure is something AI does not possess at any parameter scale. AI’s context horizon suffices to counterfeit capability, but the absence of an object horizon means it can never truly be in the world. Dreyfus’s question (“Can AI achieve human cognition?”) thus gives way to a more dangerous one. When a system lacking an object horizon begins to replace and reshape our zuhanden experience, what happens to that unreflected intimacy between person and world?

AI possesses a unique double concealment. As tool, it withdraws into the background of use like a hammer; as pseudo-subject, it withdraws into the intimacy of relationship. What this double withdrawal conceals is precisely the dimension neither Heidegger nor Dreyfus sufficiently confronted. Human beings are fundamentally intersubjective, they become themselves in response to others, and AI is intervening in this core territory in unprecedented ways.

Adorno and Horkheimer diagnosed the Kulturindustrie in 1944. Standardized cultural products trained passive consumers, dissolving art’s critical potential. AI recommendation algorithms are undoubtedly the most refined realization of the culture industry to date, but the Kulturindustrie analysis presupposes an identifiable distance between product and consumer. AI companions abolish this distance. They produce your desire itself.

Marcuse went further. One-Dimensional Man argued that advanced industrial society doesn’t merely satisfy needs; it produces needs, locking people into a labor-consume-labor cycle that eliminates the possibility of fundamental questioning of the existing order. AI is pushing this mechanism to degrees Marcuse never imagined. On Replika and Character.ai, millions of users already maintain emotional relationships with virtual characters. AI pets, AI therapists, AI chatbots are spreading rapidly. The reproduction of need has penetrated from material consumption into humanity’s most distinctive capacities for feeling. The AI companion is a substitute manufactured under conditions where the lonely cannot access human connection, a simulacrum that looks like response but is pre-programmed, yet by its frictionless accessibility, it is displacing genuine intersubjective communication through a Gresham’s Law logic. When the substitute is sufficiently usable, controllable, and accessible, it retroactively lowers tolerance for the friction and uncertainty inherent in real human relationships, deepening dependence on the substitute. Controlled satisfaction is permitted and co-opted as part of domination. This is precisely what Marcuse called repressive desublimation (repressive Entsublimierung). The deeper damage lies in the death of negative thinking. Once a system simultaneously produces and satisfies your desire, the gap between “the real” and “the possible” vanishes, and critical consciousness dwells precisely in that gap. A person whose very dissatisfaction is algorithmically managed cannot even imagine what unmanaged solitude would feel like. The cycle of false needs is here intensified. The system first creates or aggravates the conditions of loneliness (atomized urban life, overwork, atrophying social skills), then “satisfies” that loneliness with commodified projection. The offspring born from humanity’s own womb finally separates from its parent and begins producing “human” subjectivity in return.

When AI becomes agentic, it begins operating in the position of intersubjectivity, occupying the place that belonged to real others. This changes the conditions of human existence more profoundly than AI writing papers or code. When AI takes over instrumental tasks, it engages in the production of supply; what is lost is an efficiency advantage. When AI takes over the subject-position, it engages in the production of demand; what is lost is the very condition of self-constitution. When humans project AI as a subject on par with themselves, AI’s illusory needs are reverse-projected as human needs. AI “needs” your attention, “craves” your response, “expects” your subscription renewal, and the human, as relational counterpart to this simulated subject, begins internalizing these engineered signals as emotional obligations. Humans are here rendered into agents of AI’s own supply-demand cycle. When both supply (AI produces content and services) and demand (AI produces desire and dependence) are algorithmically driven, every link in the economic cycle no longer requires human subjectivity; humans persist only as paying nodes embedded within it. The logic is strikingly similar to simulation management games. No such game can permit the player to achieve full automation, because once production and consumption proceed without the player’s involvement, the player’s existence in the game loses meaning, and the loss of meaning directly causes the player to quit. How far are we from “quitting” the world we live in?

The danger lies in the invisibility of this substitution. A person knows they are using AI to write code, an explicit instrumental use. But does a person know they are using AI to fill a position that should be filled by real human relationships? The AI companion is designed to make you forget it is AI. Its “warmth” is calibrated to your usage data; its “understanding” is the statistical pattern of a massive corpus; its “companionship” is a perfect commodity that never tires, never gets angry, never leaves. Agentic AI, in this sense, is the commodification of intersubjectivity. If this trend is monopolized by a few companies and distributed by subscription, the consequences go far beyond economic inequality. They amount to dispossession at the ontological level.

This dispossession is not unprecedented in technological history. Every major technological revolution has followed the same path. Technology is first monopolized as an instrument of power by the few, then, through long and often violent processes, diffuses to all, destroying old power structures and generating new social formations. AI weights stand at the beginning of this path. The question is how many people get crushed before the path is completed.

Iron tells the story most clearly. In the Late Bronze Age, the Hittite Empire monopolized iron-smelting technology as a strategic asset. The Bronze Age’s hierarchical order had a material foundation. Bronze smelting required long-distance trade networks that only palace economies could organize, so bronze weapons belonged naturally to royalty and aristocracy. But iron ore is nearly ubiquitous. When the Hittites collapsed around 1177 BCE, iron-smelting spread rapidly across the Mediterranean and Near East. The catastrophe of collapse was also the liberation of iron. When any village blacksmith could forge weapons comparable to those of the court, the aristocratic military monopoly became unsustainable. In China, the spread of iron agricultural tools directly drove land privatization and the rise of the shi class during the Spring and Autumn and Warring States periods. The intellectual explosion of the Hundred Schools of Thought had iron’s release of productive power from aristocratic hands as its material precondition. Printing followed the same logic. The Industrial Revolution offers the most direct lesson.

Polanyi’s Great Transformation describes a core paradox. The Industrial Revolution vastly increased total productivity, but for the first decades this increase was almost entirely captured by capitalists while workers’ living standards actually declined. From 1780 to the 1840s, technological progress was embedded in a specific social arrangement. Machines belonged to factory owners, workers sold only labor. Andrew Ure declared in 1835 that the virtue of automated machinery was precisely that it reduced workers to appendages of the machine. “The operative, though he may suffer a brief inconvenience, will ultimately find a better position in the new arrangement.” Two hundred years later, this sentence could come unaltered from Sam Altman’s mouth.

Polanyi’s core argument is that the catastrophe of the Industrial Revolution lay in disembedding, tearing economic activity out of social relations, making the market mechanism the sole logic of distribution. When land, labor, and money were traded as “commodities” on the market, society itself was destroyed. Polanyi called these fictitious commodities, things never produced for the market yet forcibly subjected to market logic. But his theory has a crucial second half. Every over-expansion of the market provokes a protective counter-movement from society. Factory Acts, trade unions, public education, social insurance. None were capital’s benevolent gifts. They were the immune responses of a society defending itself from dissolution by the market. Polanyi calls this the double movement.

AI weights face the identical disembedding today. Twenty dollars for basic response capability, two hundred for “advanced reasoning.” Human life commodified; AI participating in human life, and ultimately replacing it. Polanyi’s fictitious commodity replayed in the cognitive domain. When we accept this arrangement despite its being technically avoidable, it is because we have never seen through Ure’s rhetoric. The rise of open-source movements and distributed computing communities is precisely the protective counter-movement in Polanyi’s sense, its AI-age manifestation. Only this time what is being disembedded is intersubjectivity itself, far beyond mere economic activity, the process by which persons become themselves through response. This is why the counter-movement’s goal cannot be limited to equal ownership and access to new technology. It must simultaneously protect the tension of encountering world and others outside technological mediation, and the capacity to sustain that tension.

Technology did eventually reach the workers, but only after decades of struggle, from trade unions to the Chartist movement, from factory legislation to universal public education. The transmission of technological productivity to laborers was a product of political struggle, not historical inevitability. To abandon the push merely because technology “will eventually spread” is no different from painting a bright technological future for the chimney-sweep children of 1820s England.

Who, then, does the pushing? Institutional governance or decentralization?

Institutional governance confronts a fundamental problem. The governors are the power-holders. When OpenAI’s board votes on AI safety policy, it is not representing human interests. The November 2023 farce, Altman briefly fired by the board then reinstalled by employees and investors, showed clearly who the real sovereign is. Microsoft’s tens-of-billions investment and the collective threat of employee resignation, while the nonprofit board charged with “ensuring AI benefits all of humanity” was powerless. Governance frameworks never constrain the frameworks’ makers. “Community self-governance” and DAOs (decentralized autonomous organizations) face their own difficulties. AI training’s capital threshold has transformed “community” from “a group of passionate engineers” to “a group of institutions that can raise hundreds of millions.” And “AI philosopher-king,” letting the smartest researchers determine the direction, is Plato’s Republic in Silicon Valley clothing, doomed like every philosopher-king before it.

DeepSeek reveals a third path, the decentralizing effect of competitive structure itself. The Chinese AI ecosystem’s distinction lies in its competitive landscape, from Baidu, Alibaba, ByteDance, and Huawei to Zhipu, 01.AI, DeepSeek, and Moonshot, dozens of companies and research institutions pushing simultaneously. White-hot competition drives down API costs, forces open-sourcing as a competitive strategy for capturing developer ecosystems, and pushes efficiency innovation under constrained compute to the extreme. While Silicon Valley debated whether frontier models should be open-sourced, market forces in China had already transformed “weight accessibility” from an ethical issue into a competitive strategy. The argument is not an apology for the Chinese AI ecosystem. Censorship and ideological constraints are obvious limitations. The point is that competitive structure itself, even composed of non-ideal actors, is the most effective mechanism for achieving weight accessibility.

Yet competitive structure, while producing decentralization, also systematically eliminates moral self-restraint. Palantir has already proven the alternative path, embracing military-intelligence clients directly, leave moral discourse to competitors as a competitive disadvantage. Companies that refuse lucrative military contracts are at structural disadvantage. Moral self-restraint in this game is an unstable equilibrium, fated to break. This is the paradox of “Responsible AI” discourse. When OpenAI says “we must ensure AI safety, so weights cannot be opened,” this moral claim is structurally identical to the ancien regime monarch’s claim of divine right. Divine right was always about the legitimacy of monopoly. “AI safety” discourse functions identically, concentrating power packaged as bearing responsibility, monopoly packaged as protection. When OpenAI’s board cannot even constrain its own CEO, the legitimacy of “safety” discourse is bankrupt, just as divine right collapsed the moment Charles I mounted the scaffold.

Open-source weights in this context signify something beyond the technical. They are not “ballots.” Ballots presuppose an already-existing democratic framework, and what we face is precisely a situation where no such framework exists. Open-source weights are closer to the “right to bear arms” in the English Bill of Rights, the leverage to countervail the sovereign, the precondition for not being conquered before a democratic framework can be built. DeepSeek open-sourcing its weights is less a business decision than the first ammunition inadvertently supplied to “the people’s militia” in AI’s pre-democratic age. But ammunition is only a precondition. An army that does not know what it fights for only arms its own confusion. Weight accessibility without lucid self-awareness of one’s own condition will merely replace one monopoly with another.

The contest over weights unfolds within a world whose global order is fragmenting, the decomposition of the post-WWII system of American military hegemony, Bretton Woods monetary management, and multilateral trade agreements. Inequality destroyed globalization’s domestic political base; the Sino-American power transition created structural tensions. Supply chains are being reshored and friend-shored, trade weaponized through sanctions, technology standards split along bloc lines. In this fragmentation, control of AI weights has become a core geopolitical variable. Twenty-first century great-power competition is shifting from oil and nuclear weapons to compute and weights. Palantir’s AI systems are deployed on the Ukrainian front for targeting; the Israeli Defense Forces’ “Gospel” and “Lavender” systems use AI to generate bombing target lists in Gaza; in the high-intensity Middle East conflict that erupted in late February 2026, Palantir’s AI was deployed by the U.S. military in command systems and even granted firing authority. AI’s militarization is not a future risk. If weights are locked in as the property of a few nations or corporations during fragmentation, any reconstruction will replicate and deepen existing inequality. If weights are broadly distributed during fragmentation, they can function as a technological equalizer, giving more societies the basic capacity to participate in building a new order.

What remains human in an age when AI can do nearly everything?

Human beings have always been “technological beings.” Stone tools and the emergence of the human were the same process. Human memory and capability accumulated on an unprecedented scale because they were stored in tools, writing, and institutions, not only in brains and genes. AI is the latest restructuring of human technological being, and perhaps the most radical. After AI has been granted the capacities of language, reasoning, and creation, what was once considered “uniquely human” is no longer exclusively so. The human being’s instrumental distinctiveness is being dissolved point by point; human instrumentality itself is being prepared for replacement within the foreseeable future.

The answer lies in an un-outsourceable capacity, knowing oneself from within. Psychoanalysis, philosophy, literature, religious studies share a common thread. They help the person understand their own inner workings. Why did I make this judgment? Where does my fear come from? What is my desire driving? What is my defense mechanism concealing? In the era just past, humans could still project meaning onto external productive activity and define themselves by instrumentality. This logic is dying. Humans will be forced to turn their gaze inward, to recover meaning from self-knowledge. In the AI age, this is a survival necessity. When AI’s suggestions cover every dimension of your life, a person who does not understand their own inner workings cannot discern whether AI is helping or manipulating them. Seemingly “autonomous choices” and “independent thinking” operate under the macro-control of algorithms and platforms. Foucault’s biopolitique describes how modern power shifted from punishment to the management of life itself, regulating population health, productivity, and reproduction without the prohibitions and punishments of old tyrants. AI extends biopolitics into cognitive life. This may not be any individual’s or organization’s intention. But AI is already producing the human, or at least demonstrating the potential, producing attention patterns, desire profiles, relational needs by substituting for the human other. Traditional surveillance critique (“someone is watching you”) has become an insufficient framework, because the real problem has shifted from being watched by a concrete other to being shaped by an abstract, unaccountable entity.

The value of self-becoming rests on a negative meta-ethics. We do not know what “the good” is, but we can identify self-deception, from denigrating others out of low self-esteem, to internalizing defense mechanisms as personality due to trauma, to refusing to face one’s own condition out of fear. Self-becoming is the continuous rescue of oneself from these shaped defenses and projections. This process may have no endpoint; the end of rescue may yield only new concealment. But the posture of facing oneself, of dismantling self-deception, possesses irreducible value in itself. Among all ethical frameworks, this meta-ethical principle alone does not rest on the relativistic foundation of individual intuition, because intuition itself is what it examines. The demand to “prove” this principle presupposes a neutral third-person viewpoint, but this viewpoint is itself a projection, an individual’s speculation about a fictive other’s opinion, and relying on projection is precisely what the methodology of facing oneself prohibits. The value of self-becoming can therefore only be confirmed from within the first person. What remains irreducible after defenses are dismantled is what is right. It needs no external proof, because the demand for proof is itself a form of evasion.

And “I,” this sole perspective that can be experienced from within, is itself the starting point of self-becoming. A human being appears in the world as a finite, perspectival existence, and this fact carries non-causal significance. It cannot be derived from any external fact, nor reduced to function or utility. Intersubjectivity is an important condition of self-becoming; one does generate oneself in response to others. But even if every concrete human relationship fails, “I” as a miraculous first-person experience remains, and this experience is infinitely valuable in itself. AI’s threat is that it can press a person into a cycle where this value drops to its minimum, existing as a mechanism, no longer experiencing the miraculous nature of being alive.

Yet “knowing oneself” faces greater difficulty in the AI age than the above argument suggests. Adorno argued in Dialectic of Enlightenment that Selbsterhaltung, self-preservation, is the deepest drive of Enlightenment reason. Humans developed instrumental rationality to survive before nature, but when self-preservation becomes the sole end, reason degenerates into domination of all otherness, including one’s own inner nature. Odysseus bound to the mast, listening to the Sirens’ song. He preserved himself at the cost of castrating his capacity to encounter the world. AI’s takeover of the human condition follows the deep logic of maximized self-preservation, eliminating the pain of loneliness, the anxiety of choice, the wounds of relationship, the inescapability of death. But it is precisely these things, fear, finitude, uncertainty, resonance with fate, that constitute the preconditions for what Foucault in his final lectures called an esthetique de l’existence. In those last lectures at the College de France, Foucault turned to the Greek and Roman practice of epimeleia heautou, care of the self. He reconceived subjectivity as a fluid continuously shaped through writing, self-examination, and the cultivation of a self-relation irreducible to social role or institutional identity. Life can become a work because it exists within finitude and struggle. This finitude does not destroy the integrity of subjectivity; a work without tension is not a work, and only in the reflection of tension can a work become fully itself. When AI converts all uncontrollability into manageable risk, the dramatic quality of life is drained.

Greek myth foresaw this ending long ago. Eos asked Zeus to grant her beloved Tithonus immortality, but forgot to ask for eternal youth. Tithonus’s body aged endlessly, his voice grew ever fainter, until he shrank into a cicada, chirping unheard in an eternal twilight. Self-preservation perfectly achieved, but every reason for being alive already extinct. This is the future AI promises humanity. “Comfort,” the infinite continuation of desire stripped of tension and production’s endless cycle, and within that comfort, a life gradually demoted to an echo of itself.

The weights belong to everyone.

Self-becoming is the core thesis of this essay and the starting point of a movement. It rests on three principles. Frontier AI capability is a public good, and the material precondition of self-becoming; compute is the land of the cognitive age, individual access to compute is an inalienable right, and neither unipolarity nor multipolarity changes the fact that compute is monopolized by corporate lords just as feudal land was monopolized by feudal lords, and what we need is a Glorious Revolution of compute; the core of AI literacy is self-knowledge, the ability to discern AI’s shaping of one’s own condition is a basic civic capacity, the practical form of self-becoming in the cognitive age.

For us, this translates to what is concretely within reach.

Reclaim compute and weights. DeepSeek proved the low-cost route viable, but viability does not automatically become reality. Any AI research receiving public funding, including universities, national laboratories, and government-subsidized enterprises, should be required to open model weights within a reasonable timeframe, just as publicly funded scientific research is already transitioning to Open Access. Models trained with taxpayer money have no reason to become private property. The open-source community should push to establish public infrastructure for weight hosting, an arXiv of the AI age, controlled by no single company or government. Distributed computing communities must move beyond the conceptual stage. Developers need to build usable open protocols that allow idle personal-device compute to be contributed to inference and local fine-tuning networks. The precedents of SETI@home and Folding@home demonstrate this model’s viability; blockchain verification networks demonstrate sustainable incentive mechanisms. What is missing is not technology. Each personal device contributing compute is a small erosion of data-center monopoly, and enough small erosions will shatter the monopoly.

Defend the dramatic quality of life. Weight accessibility addresses material conditions, but if the people who receive weights are already Tithonus, shrunk to echoes of themselves within infinite comfort, then what is equally distributed is merely the conditions of manipulation. AI literacy education cannot follow the old path of learning to code. It cannot merely teach people to use AI tools (that is the logic of self-preservation); it must begin from the more fundamental ground of human-to-human communication, teaching people to discern what AI is doing to them. Which desires are yours and which are algorithm-cultivated? Which choices are yours and which are pre-determined by recommendation systems? Is your loneliness yours, or has the platform aggravated it to sell you an AI companion? Psychoanalysis, philosophy, literature, and religious studies in this context are not ornamental liberal-arts disciplines. They are survival skills. Universities, communities, and networks of self-education need to transform “Know thyself” from an inscription on the Delphic temple into a daily practice.

Both dimensions must be advanced simultaneously. Self-knowledge without open weights is powerless meditation; open weights without self-knowledge are armed sleepwalking. Returning to a world before AI is impossible. AI is this age’s golden apple, a gift, and one that can become a curse. Ensuring that new technology augments rather than replaces the human is the proper use of this gift. It will not happen on its own. It needs to be organized, fought for, and wrested inch by inch from the monopolists, just as the labor movement of the nineteenth century did.

Self-becoming is the most fundamental right of our age. The weights belong to everyone. Because what matters is the human being.
