# Lies to Live On: The Dual Nature of Ideology

## The Temptation and Escape of Eternal Lies

> Because people always hope to hear the answers they want to hear,
> And cannot rest until they get the answers they seek in their hearts
> You, I want you to tell me eternal, unbreakable lies
> Never revealing the truth behind them no matter when
>
> 人はみな望む答だけを聞けるまで尋ね続けてしまうものだから
> 君よ永遠の嘘をついてくれ　いつまでもたねあかしをしないでくれ
>
> — Miyuki Nakajima, "Tell Me Eternal Lies"

Miyuki Nakajima's song "Tell Me Eternal Lies" was created in the early 1990s, a period when the Japanese left-wing movement was declining, amid the collapse of the Soviet Union and the dramatic changes in Eastern Europe. The left-wing utopian ideals that had supported the spiritual world of countless idealists came crashing down. The "eternal lies" in the song can be understood both as sweet lies in love and as the comforting narratives of ideology. When we review the turbulent history of the left-wing movements in the 20th century, we cannot help but ask: Did those revolutionary ideals that once drove countless people to risk their lives also contain a kind of "eternal lie"?

From the perspective of defense mechanisms proposed by Freud, ideology functions as a collective process of "rationalization" and "projection," providing a narrative framework that explains the sources of pain and offers hope when facing complex and cruel social realities. Marx's statement that "religion is the opium of the people" can be extended to understand that all ideologies serve to some extent as "spiritual painkillers" for society. This analogy is profound, but I must point out that Marx's critique of religion overlooked a crucial point: while narcotics may disconnect one from reality, they are also necessary for patients in severe pain. Similarly, while the "anesthetic" function of ideology hinders a clear perception of reality, it also protects humans from directly facing existential anxiety of emptiness and absurdity. The "hope for changing the world" that left-wing thought offers to people is nothing but a path of escape from reality.

This anesthetic effect of ideology is particularly evident in contemporary American political polarization. In the 2024 election cycle, both the MAGA movement led by Trump and the growing progressive forces within the Democratic Party offer some form of "eternal lies." Right-wing nationalists believe in an American past wrapped in the myth of "greatness," a pure, homogeneous, prosperous golden age that never truly existed, using the slogan "Make America Great Again" to escape the irreversible social changes brought by globalization, automation, and demographic shifts. This nostalgic nationalism is essentially a simplified escape from complex reality, an anxious response to uncontrollable changes. Trump's persistent claims about a "stolen election," despite lacking substantial evidence, provide his supporters with a psychological framework to rationalize political defeat, becoming a collective "eternal lie."

Meanwhile, "Woke Culture" at the other end, though starting as a critique of social injustice, has similarly developed its own dogmas and escape mechanisms. In its extreme form, complex social issues are simplified into a binary opposition of oppressor/oppressed, with individuals reduced to representatives of their identity categories rather than complete persons with complex personalities and circumstances. The protests surrounding the Israeli-Palestinian conflict on American campuses occupy a romantic idealistic position in public discourse similar to the anti-war waves of the sixties, but their practice is often accompanied by selective moral outrage, condemning Israeli actions while remaining relatively silent about Hamas atrocities. Ideology filters factual information, forming cognitive frameworks that conform to preset positions. "Cancel Culture," as a derivative phenomenon of woke culture, achieves control over dissent through moral condemnation and social exclusion. This modern form of "heresy judgment" not only suppresses necessary social dialogue but also provides participants with a sense of moral purity and community identity. This seemingly "liberating intolerance" may ultimately lead to another form of thought suppression. Under the reinforcement of social media algorithms, this ideological self-confirmation forms more solid information cocoons, further strengthening intellectual isolation.

In Palestinian support protests erupting in major European cities, left-wing groups often simplify the conflict into a binary opposition of "oppressors and oppressed," unintentionally ignoring the complex historical and religious contradictions in the Middle East. This simplification is both a cognitive escape from complex reality and a reinforcement of one's moral superiority. At the same time, right-wing ideology similarly avoids complex colonial history and geopolitical reality by simplifying narratives—framing the conflict as an opposition between "civilization and barbarism" or "democracy and terrorism." Both sides selectively consume information within ideological frameworks, reinforcing preset positions. Participants in these social movements often gain a sense of "moral purity" and belonging through ritualized collective protests, which precisely embodies the dual function of ideology as a psychological defense mechanism: providing an emotional outlet when facing uncontrollable global tragedies while escaping the cognitive discomfort brought by complex realities through simplified narratives.

Meanwhile, debates on issues such as abortion rights, gun control, and gender identity have long transcended the realm of rational analysis, becoming symbols of collective identity. Media platforms like Fox News and MSNBC are no longer information transmitters but reinforcers of specific ideological discourse, consolidating the worldviews of their respective camps through selective reporting and emotionalized narratives. Social media algorithms further exacerbate this polarization by prioritizing content that triggers strong emotional responses. The different interpretations of the January 6, 2021, Capitol event—from "patriotic uprising" to "attack on democracy"—demonstrate how the same event can be integrated into drastically different ideological narratives, becoming evidence for their respective "eternal lies."

For many left-wing activists, the revolutionary identity becomes a special Jungian persona, through which they escape the pain of facing the true self beneath the mask by performatively perfecting this persona. This Jungian concept helps us understand that many radical leftists' passionate commitment to ideology may actually be a performance and confirmation of self-identity. The characteristic of this performance is that the performer does not acknowledge it as a performance but views it as ultimate reality. This also explains why "heresy trials" in left-wing movements are often more severe than in conservative camps—because questioning the performance directly threatens the performer's entire self-identification system. During Stalin's Great Purge (1936-1938), carried out in the name of "eliminating enemies of the people" and "defending revolutionary achievements," almost all old Bolshevik elites from Lenin's era were eliminated. The accused were often forced to admit they were "Trotskyists" or "imperialist spies," with many still shouting loyalty to the Party and Stalin before execution. These dramatic scenes reveal how ideology profoundly reshapes individual cognition, making people prefer to believe "the Party cannot be wrong" rather than face painful truths. Similarly, the Yan'an Rectification Movement (1942-1945), though ostensibly correcting various "non-proletarian thoughts" within the Party, created a special "theatrical effect" through its mechanisms of thought reform and self-criticism: participants were both actors and audience, required to continuously "transform" themselves in collective ritualized criticism and self-criticism, performing the image of a "new person" conforming to ideological requirements. During this process, many intellectuals were suspected of being spies in the "Rescue Campaign" and forced to write numerous insincere "confession materials." Many intellectuals who were reformed during the Cultural Revolution eventually truly believed the implanted narratives and deeply repented themselves. This mechanism of "self-censorship" is actually a forced reconstruction of individual self-cognition, a denial of the true self and all other personas, and a following of the ideological "eternal lie."

## The Essential Critique and Reflection on Ideology

The setbacks in the history of left-wing movements are not merely failures of practical methods, but more deeply, some left-wing ideals themselves contradict the basic operational laws of human society. Particularly in the economic realm, the communist ideal of "from each according to his ability, to each according to his needs" ignores the fundamental contradiction between the limitlessness of human desire and the limitations of resources. Marx's profound critique of capitalism reveals its internal contradictions and alienation effects, yet his vision of communist society sidesteps the complexity of human nature itself. Given the known human tendency to seek "more," a purely egalitarian distribution system essentially denies the basic laws of economic operation. I believe the most fatal blind spot in left-wing economic thought is viewing human self-interest as a product of capitalism rather than a constant of human nature, thus incorrectly assuming this motive can be eliminated through institutional reform.

Historical experience shows that faith in planned economies essentially represents a conflict between economies that respect market laws and those attempting to override them. Market laws, as a spontaneous order, operate with an objectivity similar to natural laws—administrative power or revolutionary enthusiasm can neither light fire on ice nor turn stones into gold, nor can they create an ideal society that is both abundant and equal out of thin air. Hayek's warning in "The Road to Serfdom" has been historically validated: attempts to fully plan the economy inevitably lead to two consequences—inefficiency and power concentration. The Soviet Union's seventy-year experiment with planned economy and China's policy shift from the Great Leap Forward to Reform and Opening Up both prove that efforts to replace market self-regulation with will and planning ultimately cannot be sustained. The denial of this reality is a typical manifestation of ideology as an escape mechanism—avoiding the pain of accepting the irreconcilable contradiction between ideal and reality by denying the objective existence of economic laws.

However, blind worship of the market similarly constitutes an ideological escape. The American right wing's near-religious faith in the "free market" ignores real issues such as market failures, monopoly formation, and externalities, simplifying complex economic phenomena into a binary opposition of "government intervention vs. market freedom." After the 2008 financial crisis, despite the near-collapse of the laissez-faire financial system, Wall Street right-wing discourse still insisted on attributing the problem to "over-regulation" rather than systemic risk. This faith in the market's self-regulatory capacity is essentially a simplified narrative escaping complex reality, another form of "eternal lie."

The contradictions of contemporary American right-wing nationalism on economic issues are even more obvious. On one hand, they raise the banner of free markets; on the other, they advocate trade protectionism and economic nationalism. This isn't even a logically consistent theoretical system but an emotional response to specific anxieties. The MAGA movement's economic nationalist appeal—"bring jobs back to America"—ignores the irreversibility of globalization and technological change, a selective denial of economic reality. This unrealistic promise is effective precisely because, as an "eternal lie," it satisfies the psychological need for certainty and security among specific groups.

As we have all witnessed recently, algorithm recommendations, social media groupings, and online communities make it easier for people to immerse themselves in information cocoons formed by like-minded individuals. Both left and right wings tend to consume information that conforms to their preset positions, viewing opposing voices as products of "hostile forces" or "false consciousness." In the new media environment, ideological discourse turns public dialogue into a binary opposition of friends and enemies, with debate replaced by emotional resonance and moral superiority. This trend turns the public discourse space into a battleground of "eternal lies," exhausted by endless disputes between different self-deceptive discourse systems. More paradoxically, the tellers of lies never believe they are lying, and the listeners of lies never believe they are being deceived. This circular logic of "eternal lies" makes ideological discourse no longer a dialectic of truth and falsehood, right and wrong, but an endless cycle of self-confirmation and rejection of others. Even from a moral perspective, both producers and consumers of lies accept them in an almost forced posture, because the lies themselves support their self-identity and social belonging. Breaking these lies would be a devastating blow to the personas they rely on for survival. People who belittle others often have narcissistic tendencies, and narcissists often rely on narcissism to compensate for their inferiority—this is the same principle. The tragedy of left-wing thought lies in how its vision of liberation may lead to new fetters; its construction of the "realm of necessity" aligns with its theories describing reality and lofty moral destinations, and once reality doesn't match this alignment, self-deceptive lies become an inevitable result. How to face one's dimension as an "eternal lie" has become an urgent issue.

## Final Words: Between Lies and Truth

Desiring truth yet fearing it might destroy the meaning system we depend on for survival. Based on this pattern, each ideology inevitably creates new lies. The Marxist tradition reveals the structural contradictions between capital and labor; right-wing nationalism captures the dissolution of local identity by globalization; woke culture keenly points out the continued existence of institutional injustice, but each trend is ignorantly forced to be trapped in its own meaning system.

Perhaps what's needed to transcend the current polarized American political environment is a common examination of various "eternal lies"—whether the nostalgic phantasm of right-wing nationalism, the idealism of left-wing utopias, or the moral purism of woke politics. True courage lies not in persisting with one's "eternal lies" but in daring to examine these lies, acknowledging their limitations and illusions. What we need is not more "eternal lies" but more courage to face the truth. As Freud said, "Only by confronting pain can we gain true freedom." The greatest social problem we face today, and the warning already sounded, is precisely that line:

"Never revealing the truth behind them no matter when"
