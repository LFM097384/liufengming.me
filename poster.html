01 The Question

Douglas (2007) demonstrates that value-free science is impossible. Scientists inevitably make value judgments when assessing evidence, because the consequences of accepting a false hypothesis differ from those of rejecting a true one.

But if value-neutrality is impossible, why does the ideal persist? The answer I propose is that the ideal is not merely unattainable. It is ideological: it disguises a specific set of commitments — instrumental reason, quantifiability, formalization — as the absence of values altogether.

02 Douglas on Inductive Risk

Douglas's key insight turns on inductive risk. In deciding when evidence is sufficient, scientists must weigh the costs of different kinds of error — which is inescapably a value judgment.

The consequences of accepting a false hypothesis or rejecting a true one vary depending on what is at stake, and this requires value judgments about which errors are more tolerable. — Douglas (2007)

This argument is necessary but not sufficient. Douglas shows that values infiltrate science. She does not ask what ideological function is served by continuing to insist on value-neutrality despite its impossibility.

03 Nagel's Failed Defence

Nagel (1961) attempts to rescue objectivity by distinguishing two types of value judgment:

  • Characterizing — selecting problems, defining concepts (unavoidable)
  • Appraising — evaluating moral worth (supposedly excludable)

This distinction cannot hold. The concepts through which social science apprehends its objects already carry entire worldviews. "Rationality," "development," "efficiency" are not neutral descriptors. They encode specific conceptions of human flourishing and encode them as self-evident.

04 Core Thesis

The claim to neutrality performs three linked ideological operations:

  • It disguises a perspective as no perspective. By presenting quantification and formal methodology as "objectivity," the value-free ideal positions instrumental reason as the only legitimate way of knowing — while concealing that this is itself a substantive commitment.
  • It marginalizes rival traditions. Hermeneutics, phenomenology, and critical theory are demoted to "soft" disciplines precisely because they refuse the pretence of neutrality. Their reflexivity is treated as a deficiency rather than as intellectual honesty.
  • It mirrors liberal neutrality. Liberalism claims neutrality among conceptions of the good while enforcing a particular procedural framework (Rawls, 1971). Value-free science performs the same move in the epistemic domain: it enforces a framework while denying it is one.

05 Genealogy: Instrumental Reason

Enlightenment, understood in the widest sense as the advance of thought, has always aimed at liberating human beings from fear and installing them as masters. Yet the wholly enlightened earth is radiant with triumphant calamity. — Adorno & Horkheimer (1944)

Reason, developed to master nature, becomes a means of domination — including self-domination. The demand for "objectivity" is, at bottom, the demand that the knowing subject erase its own particularity.

Heidegger deepens this point. His concept of Gestell (enframing) describes how modern technology reveals all beings as Bestand — standing-reserve, resources awaiting deployment. Under the value-free ideal, science reveals truth itself as standing-reserve: knowledge is whatever can be operationalized.

06 Implications for Social Science

When economics models human behaviour as utility-maximisation, it is not neutrally describing. It is constructing the subject it claims to observe. Homo economicus is not discovered in nature; it is an artefact of the framework.

When political science treats "stability" as a dependent variable, it already endorses a conception of the political good — one that privileges order over justice, continuity over transformation.

The categories of social science are not neutral lenses through which we see the world. They are performative. They shape what they claim only to measure.

07 The Alternative: Value-Transparency

If value-neutrality is both impossible and ideological, what follows?

Not relativism. The conclusion is not that "all values are equal" or that "science is just politics." Giving up the pretence of neutrality does not mean giving up rigour.

The alternative is value-transparency: making explicit the values embedded in research design, evidence evaluation, and theoretical framing. This strengthens science by exposing hidden assumptions to scrutiny (Anderson, 2004).

But we must go further than transparency as a method. Genuine reflexivity demands recognising that the demand for neutrality is itself a value — and perhaps the most effectively concealed one.

Conclusion

The ideal of value-free science is not merely unattainable. It is a mechanism of ideological closure. It disguises instrumental reason as the absence of value. It marginalises epistemic traditions that practice reflexivity. It forecloses critical reflection on science's own presuppositions.

The task is not to achieve neutrality,
but to achieve reflexivity.
